What's Actually in Your Gutters

Homeowners often assume gutter debris is mostly leaves. In the eastern Pierce County foothills and throughout the Bonney Lake and Puyallup areas, the reality is more complex.

Conifer Needles

Douglas fir, Western red cedar, and Ponderosa pine mat together into a dense, water-resistant pack. Unlike leaves, needles don't break down quickly. They compress into the gutter channel and can hold enough moisture to add significant weight to the system over time.

Moss and Lichen Fragments

Shed from the roof surface, particularly on north-facing and shaded pitches. Moss in the gutter adds weight, retains moisture, and accelerates organic buildup.

Shingle Granules

Granules from asphalt shingles accumulate in gutters over the life of the roof. Heavy granule presence indicates shingle wear and is worth noting.

Seasonal Leaf and Seed Matter

Seed pods, catkins, and deciduous leaf matter from maple, alder, and ornamental trees add the seasonal layer most homeowners expect — but in PNW gutter systems, they're one component among several.

Our Cleaning Process

A-Team Gutters cleans gutters by hand and with equipment sized for the debris type present. We don't use high-pressure air blowers that push debris onto the roof or landscaping and call the job complete.

01

Debris Removal

Gutters are cleared by hand or with gutter-specific tools. Material is bagged and removed from the property.

02

Downspout Flush

Every downspout is flushed with water to clear blockages and verify that the outlet is fully open. Packed needle debris is the most common downspout blockage we encounter.

03

Flow Test

We run water through the completed system to confirm proper pitch and drainage to every downspout.

04

Condition Report

If we observe anything during cleaning that warrants attention — a pulling hanger, a cracked section, evidence of fascia moisture — we note it and let you know before we leave.

How Often Should Gutters Be Cleaned in Western Washington?

The standard recommendation for most Pacific Northwest homes is twice per year — once in late fall after the primary leaf and needle drop, and once in late winter or early spring after the heavy rain season.

Homes with significant conifer overhang may require three or four cleanings per year. Needle shed from mature Douglas firs is year-round, with a secondary heavy drop in late spring. If you're finding your downspouts backing up between scheduled cleanings, more frequent service is warranted.

A one-size-fits-all cleaning schedule doesn't account for your specific tree canopy, roof pitch, or gutter screen status. We can advise on appropriate frequency after a first cleaning and inspection.
